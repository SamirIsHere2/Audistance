OtoCorrect helps you find the right distance for a conversation, shape sound to fit your own ears, and check how you're hearing across the frequency range — all running privately in this browser tab.

Let OtoCorrect adjust in-page volume?
Turning this on lets OtoCorrect raise or lower the volume of audio played on this page (like the test tone above) based on how far you are from the camera — quieter as you move closer, louder as you move farther away.

This can't touch your computer's system volume or any other app or tab — only sound played here.
Distance is an approximation. It assumes an average adult face width (~14 cm) and a typical webcam field of view, so treat the number as a general guide rather than a clinical measurement. The default 90–150 cm (≈3–5 ft) range reflects common guidance for comfortable face-to-face hearing and lip-reading distance — adjust it above for your own setup.

Let OtoCorrect adjust Spotify volume?
Turning this on lets OtoCorrect raise or lower the playback volume on your active Spotify device based on how far you are from the camera above, using Spotify's official volume control API.

It can only change volume on the device that's currently playing — it can't start, stop, skip, or change what's playing, and it can't touch your system volume.
This equalizer is a comfort tool for exploring what frequency balance sounds clearest to you — it isn't a calibrated or clinical correction, and it doesn't process audio from other apps or tabs. Spotify integration uses Spotify's official Web API under your own developer credentials and is not affiliated with or endorsed by Spotify.
Hearing Test — self-guided check

Built-in audiogram

A short check across six frequencies (250 Hz–8 kHz), followed by ten spoken words at varying volumes. Put on headphones in a quiet room, set a comfortable volume, and click the button the moment you hear each tone. When the word portion starts, we'll ask for one-time microphone access — words are spoken aloud, never shown on screen, so just say back what you hear. Full results appear together at the end.

One pass through all six tones, played to both ears at once — good for a quick, general check. Results plot as a single line.

This is a simple, uncalibrated self-check — actual loudness depends on your device and headphone volume, so it isn't a clinical hearing test and can't diagnose hearing loss. The severity bands shown are standard clinical reference ranges, but this tool cannot verify true hearing thresholds the way calibrated audiometric equipment can. Speech recognition accuracy also varies by browser, accent, and background noise, so the word check is a rough comfort measure, not a diagnostic word-recognition score. If you have ongoing concerns about your hearing, please see an audiologist or hearing care professional.
